Introduction: A Strategic Proposal for Leadership in K-5 AI Education

This document presents a comprehensive preliminary framework for the development and implementation of a groundbreaking K–5 Artificial Intelligence (AI) curriculum. It is submitted in direct response to the request for a rough budget, a 10-module curriculum overview, and a working implementation plan. The proposal is designed to provide a robust foundation for our joint pitch to key stakeholders, including the Massachusetts Clean Energy Center (MassCEC), the public school systems of New Bedford, Taunton, and Fall River, the Center for Energy Workforce Development (CEWD), and Dr. Cynthia Brown of IWNL Energy.

Strategic Imperative and Timing

The framework outlined herein is not merely an educational initiative; it represents a strategic imperative, positioning our partnership at the forefront of a national movement. The proposed timeline, with planning discussions in August 2025 and a target rollout in Spring 2026, aligns with unparalleled precision to a new wave of federal focus on AI in education. A recent Executive Order has established aggressive deadlines for federal agencies to promote and fund AI literacy programs.

This convergence of timelines is critically significant. It transforms our proposed August 2025 meeting from a simple planning session into a pivotal opportunity to finalize a "shovel-ready" proposal, perfectly timed to capture the first wave of federal investment in this domain. The project is positioned not just as a beneficial program for Massachusetts students but as a national model for K–12 AI education that directly answers a federal call to action.

Report Structure

This report is structured to provide a complete and actionable plan. It is organized into four main sections:

  • Tiered Financial Framework: A detailed, multi-tiered budget that offers strategic flexibility for investment and scaling.
  • K-5 AI Curriculum Framework: A 10-module overview meticulously modeled on the successful MassCEC curriculum structure.
  • Hybrid Implementation and Operational Plan: A logistical blueprint for the proposed 80/20 hybrid delivery model.
  • Strategic Alignment and Stakeholder Value: An analysis of how this project aligns with national funding opportunities and the specific value proposition for each partner.

Section 1: Tiered Financial Framework

1.1 Foundational Principles of the Financial Model

The financial framework is designed to be transparent, comprehensive, and strategically flexible. All personnel costs are calculated from a baseline consulting and development rate of $100 per hour. This methodology ensures a clear and justifiable rationale for all human resource allocations. The line-item structure is adapted and expanded from the comprehensive budget analysis provided in the IWNL implementation plan.

1.2 Detailed Cost Breakdown by Project Phase

To provide a clear understanding of cash flow and resource allocation, the budget is organized into three distinct phases:

  • Phase 1: Development (Months 1-8): Encompasses all upfront costs required to create the curriculum and its supporting infrastructure. Includes instructional design, subject matter experts, content writers, video production, and initial platform setup. This phase represents the largest single investment.
  • Phase 2: Implementation (Months 6-12): Covers the direct costs associated with launching the first two pilot cohorts in Spring and Summer 2026. Primary expenditures are professional development and stipends for the initial 200 teachers, and student materials for up to 600 students in New Bedford, Taunton, and Fall River.
  • Phase 3: Scale & Support (Year 2+): Outlines the anticipated annual recurring costs required to sustain and grow the program, including licensing, ongoing teacher PD, and curriculum updates.

1.3 Tiered Investment Levels: Core, Enhanced, and Premier Options

A tiered financial model is proposed to offer flexibility, transforming the budget from a simple price tag into a set of strategic choices.

  • Tier 1: Core Program (MVP): Delivers a high-quality educational experience on a lean budget. Ideal for initial pilot grants and proof of concept. Utilizes cost-effective solutions like AI-generated video and standard LMS features.
  • Tier 2: Enhanced Program (Recommended): The recommended level for the proposed rollout. Invests in higher production values (custom animation), a robust platform, and intensive teacher PD. Designed as a flagship program for Massachusetts.
  • Tier 3: Premier National Model: Positions the project for national recognition and large-scale federal funding. Includes bespoke production, a premium platform with advanced analytics, a "train-the-trainer" model, and budget for a longitudinal impact study.

Section 2: K-5 AI Curriculum Framework

2.1 Curriculum Design Philosophy

The pedagogical approach is grounded in a commitment to fostering curiosity, critical thinking, and a foundational understanding of the technologies shaping our world. The curriculum is built on four core principles:

  • Inquiry-Based Learning: Each module is driven by an essential question.
  • Computational Thinking: Activities develop skills such as pattern recognition, decomposition, and algorithmic thinking, often in "unplugged" contexts.
  • Early Ethics and Societal Impact: Introducing concepts of fairness, bias, and responsible use of technology from the earliest grades.
  • Career Pathway Connection: Illuminating the connection between AI concepts and future career opportunities.

The curriculum's format is modeled directly on the successful MassCEC "Massachusetts Climate Careers" curriculum. This structure emphasizes real-world connections and explicit links to career pathways, including "AI-Critical Occupations Referenced" in every module.

2.2 Module-by-Module Breakdown

The following is a detailed overview of the 10 modules.

Module 1: What is AI Around Us?

Grade Level: K–1 | Essential Question: Where do we see AI helping us every day?

Lesson Summary: This foundational lesson introduces AI not as a complex concept, but as a helpful tool (voice assistants, recommendations) that is already part of students' world. The goal is to build familiarity and demystify "AI."

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: AI Product Tester, User Experience (UX) Designer, Animator.

Module 2: Computers Have Senses Too

Grade Level: K–1 | Essential Question: How do computers "see," "hear," and understand the world?

Lesson Summary: By connecting computer sensors (cameras, microphones) to their own senses, students develop a tangible mental model for how machines perceive the physical world (input/output).

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Robotics Technician, Computer Vision Engineer (conceptual), Sound Engineer.

Module 3: Teaching Computers to Learn

Grade Level: 1–2 | Essential Question: How can we teach a computer to recognize things?

Lesson Summary: Introduces the core concept of machine learning in an accessible way. Students understand that AI learns from many examples (training data) and that AI performance depends on the data it is given.

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Data Labeler, Machine Learning Engineer (conceptual), Teacher (for AI).

Module 4: Patterns and Predictions

Grade Level: 2–3 | Essential Question: How does AI use patterns to make predictions?

Lesson Summary: This lesson moves from recognition to prediction. Students gain an intuitive understanding of how recommendation systems and other predictive technologies work by identifying patterns themselves.

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Data Analyst, Market Researcher, Meteorologist.

Module 5: AI Helpers and Decisions

Grade Level: 3–4 | Essential Question: How does AI help people make better decisions?

Lesson Summary: This module introduces a nuanced view of AI's role (augmentation vs. automation). Students learn that AI is often most powerful when working in partnership with a human expert, emphasizing human oversight.

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Doctor/Clinician, Financial Advisor, City Planner.

Module 6: Creating with AI

Grade Level: 3–4 | Essential Question: How can AI help us be more creative?

Lesson Summary: Introduces the field of generative AI. Students experience AI not just as an analytical tool, but as a creative collaborator, using tools to generate ideas for art, music, or storytelling.

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Graphic Designer, Musician/Composer, Author, Game Designer.

Module 7: The Data Detective

Grade Level: 4–5 | Essential Question: How does the quality of data affect what an AI can do?

Lesson Summary: A critical lesson moving into the ethical dimension of AI. Students learn the "garbage in, garbage out" principle and begin to understand that biased data leads to biased and unfair AI systems.

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Data Scientist, AI Ethicist, Survey Researcher, Quality Assurance Analyst.

Module 8: AI Ethics and Fairness

Grade Level: 4–5 | Essential Question: How can we make sure AI is fair and helpful for everyone?

Lesson Summary: Directly addresses societal implications. Students engage in real-world ethical dilemmas, developing critical thinking around fairness, accountability, and transparency. (Activity: "Classroom AI Bill of Rights").

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: AI Ethicist, Public Policy Advisor, Community Organizer, User Advocate.

Module 9: Building Simple AI

Grade Level: 5 | Essential Question: How can we use code to create our own simple AI?

Lesson Summary: Empowers students to move from consumers to creators. By building a simple AI system using block-based programming (e.g., Scratch), they demystify the process and solidify their understanding.

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Software Developer, Game Developer, AI Programmer, Quality Assurance Tester.

Module 10: AI for Good: Problem Solvers

Grade Level: 5 | Essential Question: How can we use AI to help solve real-world problems in our community?

Lesson Summary: This capstone module synthesizes all learning. Students apply their technical knowledge and ethical understanding to design an "AI for Good" solution for a local community problem.

AI-Critical Occupations Referenced: Social Entrepreneur, Product Manager, Urban Planner, Environmental Scientist.

Section 3: Hybrid Implementation and Operational Plan

3.1 The 80/20 Hybrid Learning Model: Fusing Flexibility with High-Touch Support

The proposed implementation model leverages the scalability and flexibility of online learning (80%) while retaining the invaluable benefits of in-person instruction (20%). This is a pedagogical strategy, not just a logistical arrangement.

This structure is critical for the target districts (New Bedford, Taunton, and Fall River). The in-person session becomes the primary vehicle for equity and access, providing dedicated time for differentiated instruction, support for English Language Learners, and hands-on, collaborative projects under direct guidance.

Weekly Schedule Outline:

  • Monday (Online): Synchronous kickoff (30 min) introducing the essential question, followed by asynchronous exploration (90 min).
  • Tuesday (Online): Asynchronous project work (120 min) with teacher "office hours."
  • Wednesday (Online): Synchronous small-group collaboration (45 min), followed by asynchronous practice (75 min).
  • Thursday (Online): Asynchronous work focused on preparation for in-person session and formative assessments (120 min).
  • Friday (In-Person, 4 hours): Community Building, Hands-On/Unplugged AI Activities, Group Presentations & Peer Feedback, Assessment.

3.2 Technology and Platform Ecosystem

  • Primary Platform (Hybrid Approach): Calvert Learning Edmentum (core curriculum delivery, tracking) and Create & Learn (specialized, synchronous instruction).
  • Supplementary Platforms: Google Classroom, Scratch/Scratch Jr. (block-based programming), Interactive AI Tools (e.g., Google's Teachable Machine, Quick, Draw!).
  • Technical Requirements: 1:1 device-to-student ratio, minimum internet bandwidth (1 Mbps/student), and COPPA compliance. Equity support (device lending, hotspots) must be in place.

3.3 Teacher Professional Development and Support

The success of the curriculum hinges on teacher confidence. A comprehensive, multi-phase PD program is essential.

  • Phase 1: Summer Intensive (40 hours): Mandatory, paid training covering AI concepts, platform mastery, and hybrid pedagogy.
  • Phase 2: Implementation Support: Continuous support, including monthly PD sessions and a peer observation/coaching program.
  • Phase 3: Continuous Improvement: Fostering a Professional Learning Community (PLC), annual 20-hour summer refreshers, and quarterly updates.

3.4 Project Implementation Timeline

The proposed timeline is ambitious yet achievable, targeting a rollout in 2026.

  • Phase 1: Planning (Jan–Mar 2026): Finalizing contracts, recruiting the first teacher cohort, and conducting infrastructure assessments.
  • Phase 2: Spring Cohort Launch (Apr–Jun 2026): Initial pilot with ~200 students. Intensive support and continuous feedback loop for rapid refinement.
  • Phase 3: Summer Cohort Expansion (Jul–Aug 2026): Refined curriculum deployed to ~400 students. Launch of teacher mentorship program.
  • Phase 4: Full Implementation (Sep 2026+): Full integration into partner districts. Focus shifts to sustainability and expanding the "train-the-trainer" model.

4.2 A Tailored Value Proposition for Each Partner

This project is designed to deliver distinct and significant value to each stakeholder, creating a powerful coalition for success.

For the Massachusetts Clean Energy Center (MassCEC):

Represents an innovative extension of MassCEC's leadership in K-12 workforce development. By building on the successful model of the clean energy curriculum, MassCEC can leverage existing expertise to expand into the next critical technology sector, preparing the future workforce for the integration of AI within the clean energy industry (e.g., smart grids, predictive maintenance).

For New Bedford, Taunton, and Fall River School Systems:

Offers an opportunity to provide a cutting-edge, standards-aligned curriculum at potentially no cost (assuming grant funding). The 80/20 hybrid model is explicitly designed with their needs in mind, providing essential, high-touch support to address the needs of diverse learners.

For the Center for Energy Workforce Development (CEWD):

Directly addresses CEWD's mission by building the foundational AI literacy essential for the future energy workforce. By supporting this K-5 program, CEWD is investing in the long-term talent pipeline needed to thrive in an AI-driven industry.

For Dr. Cynthia Brown and IWNL Energy:

Provides a platform to create a scalable, sustainable, and nationally significant educational model. Participation cements IWNL's role as a thought leader and creates a powerful case study for leveraging industry expertise to address critical skills gaps.
